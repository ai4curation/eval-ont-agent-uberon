## Summary

This PR fixes taxon constraint violations that were causing three imported terms to be unsatisfiable:
- FMA:77600
- ZFA:0000497

## Changes

### UBERON:0034714 — epiphyseal tract

**Problem**: The logical definition `EquivalentTo 'cranial neuron projection bundle' and (innervates some 'parietal organ')` was too narrow. The pineal tract (UBERON:0034715, xref FMA:77600) is a branching part of the epiphyseal tract and innervates the pineal body, which is part of the pineal complex but distinct from the parietal organ. This meant the epiphyseal tract innervated more than just the parietal organ, making the equivalence axiom inconsistent.

**Fix**:
- Text definition updated from "A cranial nerve fiber tract that innervates the parietal eye." to "A cranial nerve fiber tract that innervates the pineal complex."
- Logical definition updated: `intersection_of: innervates UBERON:0015238 ! pineal complex` (was UBERON:0004869 ! parietal organ)
- Added `term_tracker_item` linking to issue #3596

### UBERON:0011144 — adductor muscle of hip

**Problem**: This term exhibited a "loose definition, tight classification" pattern. Its logical definition (`EquivalentTo 'adductor muscle' and (part_of some 'pelvic complex')`) applies to all vertebrates. However, it was explicitly classified as `innervated_by some 'obturator nerve'`, which is tetrapod-specific. This caused teleost-specific subclasses (including those mapped to ZFA:0000497 and ZFA:0000592) to inherit tetrapod-only constraints and become unsatisfiable.

**Fix**:
- Removed the `relationship: innervated_by UBERON:0005465 ! obturator nerve` axiom
- Added `term_tracker_item` linking to issue #3596
- The logical definition was preserved to maintain broad vertebrate applicability

## Rationale

I chose **option (B)** for the adductor muscle of hip (broad definition, remove tight tetrapod-specific classification) because it is consistent with UBERON's design goal of being a cross-species anatomy ontology. Removing the obturator nerve innervation constraint allows the term to correctly group homologous structures across vertebrates without forcing teleost terms into an unsatisfiable state.
