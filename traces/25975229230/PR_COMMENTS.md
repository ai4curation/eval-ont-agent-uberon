## Rationale

The carotid body is a sensory organ composed of chemoreceptor cells that detect changes in pH, carbon dioxide, and oxygen concentrations in the blood. While it is anatomically located at the bifurcation of the common carotid artery (in the adventitia), this proximity does not make it structurally part of the arterial system.

**Key points**:
1. **Function**: The carotid body serves as a chemoreceptive sensory organ in the peripheral nervous system
2. **Composition**: It consists of chemoreceptor cells (type I and II cells), not vascular tissue
3. **Location vs. Identity**: Being adjacent to the carotid artery does not mean it is part of the artery - it is a distinct sensory structure
4. **Current definition**: The existing definition correctly describes it as "a chemoreceptive organ that senses the pH, carbon dioxide, and oxygen concentrations in the blood"
5. **Supporting evidence**: PMID:32965908 (referenced in the issue) confirms this classification

The term already had `is_a: UBERON:0034979 ! nonchromaffin paraganglion`, which is consistent with its role as a peripheral nervous system sensory structure. The previous `part_of` relationship to the cardiovascular system was based on anatomical proximity rather than functional or structural identity.
